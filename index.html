but computers do more than add numbers...

  • Many of the hardest computational problems are simulations of nature.

  • What about exact simulations of nature?
    • To describe nature exactly we need quantum mechanics.

Why is simulating quantum mechanics difficult?

Superposition

A fundamental property of the very small world.

  • The only results we can measure are spin-up or spin-down
  • Before measurement it can exist in a combination (superposition) of -up and -down.

$\left| \psi \right\rangle = a \left| \downarrow \right\rangle + b \left| \uparrow \right\rangle$

  • $\left|a\right|^2$ and $\left|b\right|^2$ are the probabilities of measuring spin -down and -up

Superposition -- An Analogy*

$\left| \psi \right\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left| LAMB \right\rangle + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left| LOBSTER \right\rangle$

  • What happens when the waiter asks (measures) what you would like?
  • Your uncertain order (wavefunction) collapses into a single decision.
  • If this scenario could be recreated over and over -- 50% of the time you choose lamb, 50% lobster

* without any dead cats

more is different -- and may be impossible

  • A system of N spins has $2^N$ possible configurations
  • Any simulation must keep track of all of these configurations to determine most probable measurement result
  • Adding another electron doubles the processing power needed

increasing processing power

transistor size limits

  • Modern CPU has 10 billion ($10^{10}$) transistors.
  • Supercomputers use 100,000 processors in parallel.
    • About $10^{15}$ transistors in total.
    • Number of cells in 10 people.
  • Probability of electron(s) spontaneously hopping across $\sim e^{-kL}$
  • L = channel length
  • k ~ electron energy

power consumption limits

  • Today's supercomputers: $10^{17}$ operations per second
  • 1 billion ($10^{9}$) operations per Watt of power
  • Supercomputer consumes roughly $10^{15}$ Joules per year
    • World produces about $10^{21}$ Joules per year
  • $2.5 million CAD in electricity per year

talking our way out of the problem

If simulating quantum mechanics on a classical computer is too costly, build a quantum computer.

Quantum analogue of the transistor: a quantum bit

  • Always 0 or 1
    • Recall: channel open or closed
  • Qubit can be in any combination (superposition) of 0 and 1
  • Measuring the result collapses the state to a single outcome.

A quantum processor needs more than one qubit

  • Qubits can be combine to make more complicated superpositions
  • Multiple qubits forming a single quantum superposition are entangled.

$$\left| \psi \right\rangle = a_0 \left| 000 \right\rangle + a_1 \left| 100 \right\rangle + a_2 \left| 010 \right\rangle + a_3 \left| 001 \right\rangle + \\ a_4 \left| 110 \right\rangle + a_5 \left| 101 \right\rangle + a_6 \left| 011 \right\rangle + a_7 \left| 111 \right\rangle$$

  • A logical operation (recall AND/OR/XOR) can change this entangled state.
  • We invented a quantum computer!

A sensibly sized simulation of nature

  • Entangled state of N-qubits is a superposition of all $2^N$ configurations.
  • $$\left| \psi \right\rangle = a_0 \left| 000 \right\rangle + a_1 \left| 100 \right\rangle + a_2 \left| 010 \right\rangle + a_3 \left| 001 \right\rangle + \\ a_4 \left| 110 \right\rangle + a_5 \left| 101 \right\rangle + a_6 \left| 011 \right\rangle + a_7 \left| 111 \right\rangle$$
  • Quantum computer needs ~N qubits to simulate N quantum mechanical objects.
  • ~50 qubits can do something impossible for a classical computer!
